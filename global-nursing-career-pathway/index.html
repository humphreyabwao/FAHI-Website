U.S. employment-based immigration: key terms (nurses & CNAs)

The blocks below summarize common terms used in EB-3 employment-based sponsorship. Processes change; always verify current USCIS and Department of State guidance and seek advice from a qualified immigration attorney for your situation.

Visa category & employer petitions

EB-3 (Employment-Based Third Preference)

A green-card category for skilled workers, professionals, and certain “other” workers. Nurses and CNAs are often discussed under EB-3, but the exact sub-category and whether PERM labor certification is required depend on role, licensure, and employer sponsorship strategy.

  • Registered Nurses (RNs): Typically fall under the skilled-worker track. Many RN cases benefit from Schedule A designation, which can streamline Department of Labor steps that otherwise apply in PERM—your legal team will confirm how Schedule A applies to your petition.
  • CNAs / unlicensed roles: Often classified under “other workers,” usually requiring a full PERM labor certification to show no qualified U.S. worker is available.

PERM (Permanent Labor Certification)

A Department of Labor process where the employer tests the U.S. labor market and documents that no qualified U.S. worker is available for the role. Commonly required for CNAs and many positions under the EB-3 “other workers” category. RN cases may follow a different route when Schedule A applies.

I-140 petition (Immigrant Petition for Alien Worker)

Filed by the sponsoring employer to establish the permanent job offer and begin the employment-based green-card stage for the beneficiary. Both RN and CNA routes typically involve an I-140 once prerequisite recruitment or Schedule A steps are addressed.

Dates, bulletin & queues

Priority date

The date that fixes your place in line for an immigrant visa number. For many EB-3 cases, it is the date USCIS receives an approvable I-140. For PERM-based CNA tracks, the priority date may align with when the labor certification was filed—confirm with your petition notices.

Visa Bulletin

A monthly U.S. Department of State publication listing cut-off dates by chargeability country and category. Your priority date must be “current” (before the published cut-off) before you can move to final visa processing steps.

Cut-off date

The benchmark date in the Visa Bulletin. If your priority date is earlier than the cut-off for your category and country, you may proceed to the next stage; if not, you wait until the dates advance.

Retrogression

When visa demand exceeds annual limits, cut-off dates move backward. Priority dates that were “current” can suddenly require waiting again—plan financially and professionally for possible delays.

NVC, visa interview & arrival

Welcome letter / NVC case creation

After I-140 approval and visa availability, the National Visa Center may issue correspondence with your NVC case number and invoice IDs so you can pay fees and upload civil documents before interview scheduling.

DS-260

The online immigrant visa application completed through the Consular Electronic Application Center (CEAC). It must be submitted (and often reviewed) before your consular interview.

I-864 (Affidavit of Support)

A binding sponsorship form showing the petitioner (and joint sponsors, if used) can support the immigrant above statutory income thresholds. Employers or family sponsors complete this as required by the case.

Medical examination

A panel-physician exam required for immigrant visas, covering communicable diseases, vaccination history, and other health grounds of inadmissibility. Results are submitted sealed per consulate instructions.

Consular processing

The route where you attend an interview at a U.S. embassy or consulate (e.g., Nairobi) and receive an immigrant visa foil to enter the United States—common when the beneficiary is outside the U.S. If you are lawfully in the U.S. already, your counsel may recommend adjustment of status (Form I-485) instead.

Green Card (Permanent Resident Card)

Issued after admission on an immigrant visa (or approved adjustment). It proves lawful permanent residence and work authorization, with a path—after meeting residency rules—to apply for U.S. citizenship if desired.

Agencies & recruiter partners

USCIS (U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services)

The agency that adjudicates many petitions filed within the United States, including I-140 for employment-based cases and I-485 adjustment applications for eligible applicants already inside the U.S.

Priority worker recruiters (examples)

International staffing and recruiting firms such as MedPro, Avant, Conexus, and AMN / OGP frequently sponsor experienced RNs under EB-3. They often coordinate NCLEX readiness, English testing, employer matching, I-140 filing, and NVC scheduling—but timelines, contracts, and fees vary; review any agreement carefully with legal counsel.
